The Cure for Cancer

Treating lymphoma is a very complex issue. Lymphocytes are currently being prevented from multiplying using precision chemotherapy via photodynamic therapy (PDT). However, one issue faced when using PDT is the lack of intensity. To solve this, we propose using upconversion nanoparticles (UCNPs) to increase the intensity of light when targeting cancerous cells.

The Problem

Lymphoma

Lymphoma is a type of cancer that affects more than 30% of people aged 45 and above, with over 20,000 people dying without treatment in the US alone.

PDT

Photodynamic therapy (PDT) is a modern way of precision chemotherapy using ultraviolet light. The light is absorbed by a photosensitizing molecule to cause a reaction which attacks a cancerous system.

Problem faced

There are two types of lymphocytes: Hodgkin and Non-Hodgkin cells. Hodgkin cells multiply as the cancer spreads and are responsible for fighting other infections. Thus, killing them would result in further damage to the immunity of the body.

Intensity

What we aim to do is to integrate different chemotherapy techniques to find the perfect tradeoff between precision and intensity of PDTs.

Our Solution

PhotUP is a proposition that integrates Upconversion nanoparticles (UCNPs) into the design of Photodynamic therapy. We use the concept of Near-Infrared or NIR.

How it works

Nanoparticles capable of carrying a photosensitizing molecule within a drug due to their size and mobility in the human body will get excited to higher levels of energy. This maneuvers the ROS reaction, but at higher intensities, striking the perfect tradeoff between the power of the activated light and the surface area of the tumor.

Our CAD

We've designed a mockup for our UCNP nanocarrier that is systematically efficient in drug encapsulation. To maximize intensity without killing off a million healthy cells and damaging the immune system further, that the therapy has to be designed in a way that provides a stable environment to the photosensitizer. Our carrier has been designed to optimize the photon pathway of near-infrared light, corresponding to a wavelength right outside the visible region.

  • 3 rotors oriented in a way that allows for direct linear and peripheral navigation through tissue.
  • Payload at the top dome carrying the photosensitizing molecule, which is dispatched to the Hodgkin-immuno multiplier site before activation by an intense laser of the NIR spectrum category.
  • The payload leaves the bottom of the hole and is directly deployed on the tumors.

Cost

A specific cost can't be given to this mechanism because it depends severely on the properties of the PS molecule that allow for varied absorption of light. Different stages of lymphoma would require different activation intensities, and so when dealing with NIR photon irradiation, it becomes important to put the risk of the patient's healthy cells dying to an absolute minimum.

The cost range for 1 sub-cycle of precision photodynamic therapy with UCNPs would fall around $300 to $1000, which is around a twelfth of the average cost of chemotherapy in the United States.
